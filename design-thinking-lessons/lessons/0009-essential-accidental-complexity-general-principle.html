Which cost is the problem's, and which is yours?

The whole-Card "use client" mistake and the accidental data waterfall are the same kind of cost — one you added, not one the problem demanded.

Where this comes from

Lesson 4's oversized client bundle and Lesson 5's sequential-by-accident waterfall share something: neither cost was required by the actual problem (show a follow button; show artist and albums). Both were self-inflicted by how the code happened to be written. Fred Brooks gave this distinction a name in 1986, and it still holds.

The general version: essential vs. accidental complexity

In No Silver Bullet, Brooks splits software difficulty into two kinds. Essential complexity comes from the problem itself — inherent to what you're actually trying to do, and no architecture choice removes it, only manages it. Accidental complexity comes from the tools, techniques, and choices you brought to the problem — and, unlike essential complexity, it can genuinely be eliminated. No Silver Bullet — Fred Brooks (1986) →, primary source, read directly from the original tech report rather than a summary.

A checkout flow that must handle payment failures, partial fulfillment, and currency conversion has real essential complexity — that difficulty is the problem's, and it doesn't go away no matter how you architect it. A whole Card subtree marked "use client" so one button can useState is pure accidental complexity — nothing about "show a follow button" required shipping the avatar's JS to the browser. The waterfall in Lesson 5 is the same: nothing about "show an artist and their albums" required the second request to wait for the first.

The discipline for deciding what to fix: premature optimization

Knowing accidental complexity exists doesn't mean chasing all of it immediately. Donald Knuth, 1974: "We should forget about small efficiencies, say about 97% of the time: premature optimization is the root of all evil. Yet we should not pass up our opportunities in that critical 3%." The full quote matters — it's not an argument against optimizing, it's an argument against optimizing before you know which 3% is real. This is exactly what Lesson 4's Core Web Vitals are for: they tell you where the actual 3% is, instead of guessing.
